These are decision points and observations we're flagging now and will discuss in the writeup. I'll keep adding to this list as we hit more.
1. PC conditioning depth cap (decided ~2.5 weeks in).
We ran PC + Fisher-Z without a depth cap and it failed to terminate within 12 minutes on 76 variables × 5000 samples. We capped depth at 2 (or 3, depending on what we settle on). Two reasons:

Statistical power. With binary-ish sparse data, conditioning sets larger than 2–3 create strata so sparse that the CI test has no data per stratum. The test returns noise rather than signal.
Combinatorial cost. At depth d, each edge requires C(k, d) CI tests where k is the local neighborhood size. For k ≈ 20–30 (plausible given likely hub features in our data), depth ≥ 4 is intractable. Depth 2 keeps it tractable while capturing the dependencies that matter.

This is a methodological choice, not a bug. The PC literature recommends capping depth for moderate-dimensional sparse data; we follow that. The tradeoff: we may miss some edges that would only have been removed by conditioning on larger sets. This is honestly noted as a limitation.
2. Fisher-Z misspecification on SAE feature data.
Fisher-Z assumes joint Gaussianity. SAE feature activations are zero-inflated, heavy-tailed, and non-negative — about as far from Gaussian as it gets. We run it anyway as a fast baseline, but the more honest comparison comes from running PC with the G-test on binarized data. The agreement (or disagreement) between Fisher-Z and G-test results is itself a finding — it quantifies how much the distributional assumption matters.
